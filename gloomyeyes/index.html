Official Imagery
The Premise

A World Without Sun

Long ago, the sun retreated into the mountains. Without its light, the land rotted — forests weakened, animals vanished, and the delicate balance between humans and nature collapsed. From that darkness, zombies emerged: not monsters, but consequences.

Under the iron rule of The Uncle, fear was weaponised into hatred. Survivors were imprisoned underground. The last of the zombies — you, The Last Standing — await execution.

One chance remains. A quiet young zombie named Gloomy is waiting above. And somewhere beneath the mountain, the sun still breathes.

Dramatic Structure

Three-Act Structure

The journey of The Last Standing broken down into classical dramatic structure — setup, confrontation, and resolution.

I

Setup — The World in Darkness

Intro + Scenes 01–03

The world is broken. The sun has retreated underground, zombies have emerged as consequences of ecological collapse, and The Uncle rules through fear and extermination. The audience — zombie prisoners awaiting execution — are freed by Nina and led to the surface by Gloomy.

Inciting Incident

Nina strikes a guard, seizes the key, and opens the door. "Someone is waiting for you above."

World Established

Gloomy's home reveals his isolation — drawings of Nina cover the walls. The Wild Boar brings warmth. The Gravekeeper narrates and guides.

Act I Turning Point

The Gravekeeper delivers the news: Nina has been captured. The group must board the Uncle's train to rescue her. The mission changes from escape to infiltration.

Dramatic Function

Establishes the world, introduces the characters, sets the stakes. The audience transitions from passive prisoners to active participants with a clear goal: save Nina, survive.

II

Confrontation — Light and Darkness Collide

Scenes 04–06

The group infiltrates the Uncle's train, rescues Nina, and discovers the truth about the sun. But every victory is met with a devastating blow. The Uncle is always one step behind, and his power grows more violent with each encounter.

Rising Action

Disguised as humans, the group navigates carriage after carriage. The Uncle sniffs the air — "Zombie" — and the cover nearly shatters. Survival becomes physical: a narrow ledge on the outside of a moving train.

Midpoint — The Bridge

Gloomy and Nina's hands touch on a broken bridge. Light spreads, the structure rebuilds, flowers bloom. This is the story's emotional centre: proof that human-zombie connection can literally rebuild the world.

Act II Crisis — Gloomy Falls

Pacha reveals the sun lives underground. She teaches the ritual. Then the Uncle breaks in and drains the energy from Gloomy's eyes. Gloomy collapses. The group barely escapes through a portal. The mission's hope dies with him.

Dramatic Function

Escalation, revelation, and devastation. The audience learns HOW the world can be saved — and immediately watches the key to that salvation be destroyed. The highest hope and the lowest despair happen in the same act.

III

Resolution — Awakening

Scenes 07–08 + Outro

"Gloomy is gone." Nina refuses to stop. The world collapses physically and emotionally. In the depths of the mountain, the final confrontation explodes — and the audience must choose to act. Without their collective will, the sun stays buried forever.

Dark Night of the Soul

Scene 07. Fog, cold, silence. Gloomy is believed dead. The world shrinks to miniature — fragile, breakable. Glowing footprints are the only sign that something survives. The mountain begins to collapse around them.

Climax

Gloomy returns through a portal — eyes burning red. The fight is violent. The Uncle strikes Nina down. Something breaks in Gloomy. He charges, throws the Uncle into the abyss, and collapses. Nina raises her hands. The audience follows. Fireflies pour into Gloomy. The sun rises.

Resolution

"This world is fragile. It always was. But today, it lives. For now." Light spreads. Birds sing. Morning arrives for the first time in living memory.

Dramatic Function

Everything breaks so that it can be rebuilt. The audience's collective action — raising their hands together — is the story's thesis made physical: only together, human and zombie, can the world find its light.

Brutal Honesty

Story Analysis & Feedback

No sugar-coating. These are the structural weaknesses, character problems, and narrative gaps that need addressing before this story can reach its full emotional potential.

Critical — Breaks the story Major — Weakens impact significantly Opportunity — Could elevate the story
CRITICAL

Gloomy Has No Arc

The Problem: Gloomy starts brave, stays brave, dies brave, returns brave. He's silent the entire time. He never doubts, never fails, never chooses wrong. He's a plot device wearing a character's skin.

Why It Hurts: He's the title character. If the audience doesn't feel HIS internal journey, his death in Scene 06 has no weight, and his resurrection feels like a reset button — not a transformation.

Fix

Give Gloomy a flaw. Maybe he's afraid of humans — not just quiet, but actively terrified. His drawings of Nina aren't love, they're obsession born from isolation. When he meets the audience (fellow zombies), he doesn't immediately trust them either. His arc: learning that connection requires vulnerability, not just courage. His "death" should come from a moment where he finally opens up — and it costs him everything. His resurrection then means something: he chose connection over safety, and the world rewarded him for it.

CRITICAL

Nina Is Too Perfect

The Problem: Nina is brave, empathetic, rebellious, and right about everything — from Scene 01 to Scene 08. She never fails, never doubts, never makes a choice that costs her. She's a walking thesis statement, not a person.

Why It Hurts: Perfect characters are boring. If Nina is always right, the audience has no reason to worry about her. Without worry, there's no tension. Without tension, her "rescue" in Scene 05 is just moving a chess piece.

Fix

Give Nina a real failure. Maybe in Scene 01, her escape plan gets someone killed — a zombie prisoner who trusted her doesn't make it. That guilt follows her. In Scene 05, when she throws Gloomy to the floor before recognizing him, make that violence come from a darker place — she's been brutalised by the Uncle's imprisonment and has become harder, less trusting. Her arc: regaining the empathy she claims to have but has been losing. The final hand-raising isn't just courage — it's her choosing to trust again despite everything.

CRITICAL

The Uncle Is Cardboard

The Problem: He's evil. He's paranoid. He's narcissistic. And that's all we know. We never learn WHY he hates zombies, WHY he's possessive of Nina (is she his niece? his daughter? a stranger he adopted?), or what he actually WANTS beyond "control." He's a collection of villain adjectives, not a character.

Why It Hurts: A villain without motivation is just an obstacle with a face. The audience can't fear what they don't understand. And they definitely can't feel the weight of his defeat if he was never more than a cartoon tyrant.

Fix

The Uncle needs a wound. Maybe he was the one who caused the sun to retreat — not on purpose, but through an experiment or act of hubris. His entire regime is built on covering up his guilt. He exterminates zombies because they're living proof of his failure. He hoards sun energy because he's trying to fix what he broke — but selfishly, privately, only for himself. His possessiveness over Nina: she's the last person who believed in him before the fall, and he can't let her see what he's become. Now his defeat is tragic, not just satisfying.

MAJOR

The Uncle Teleports

The Problem: The Uncle is in the dungeons (Scene 01), on the train (Scene 04), at Pacha's sanctuary (Scene 06), in the mountains (Scene 07), and in the Sun Cavern (Scene 08). He appears wherever the plot needs him with no explanation of how he gets there.

Why It Hurts: It makes the world feel like a set, not a place. If the villain can appear anywhere at any time, running from him is pointless — which kills the tension of every escape scene.

Fix

Either give the Uncle a tracking method (he implanted something in the zombies during their imprisonment, or his device can detect sun energy and he follows its trail), or show him arriving — let the audience hear his train in the distance, see his soldiers scouting ahead. Make his pursuit feel like a hunt, not a magic trick. Even better: have him NOT appear in Scene 06. Let a lieutenant or the pets attack Pacha's cave. Save the Uncle for the final confrontation. Less appearances = more impact.

MAJOR

Portals Are Deus Ex Machina

The Problem: A portal opens in Scene 05 (no explanation). Nina opens a portal in Scene 06 (since when can she do that?). Portals open and close everywhere in Scene 07. Gloomy falls through a portal in Scene 08. There are zero rules for how portals work, who can create them, or what they cost.

Why It Hurts: Every time a portal saves the characters from danger, it cheapens the danger. "They're surrounded? Portal. Dying? Portal. Need Gloomy back? Portal." It's a magic escape hatch that removes all stakes.

Fix

Establish portal rules early. Maybe they're a natural phenomenon — tears in reality caused by the sun's absence, like earthquakes in reverse. They're unpredictable and dangerous. In Scene 05, the portal that appears is luck — and stepping through it has a cost (they lose track of Pacha's location, or someone gets separated). In Scene 06, Nina doesn't "open" a portal — she recognises one forming and makes the call to jump, knowing they might end up somewhere worse. Remove portals from Scene 08 entirely. Gloomy should walk back in, not fall from the sky.

MAJOR

The Human-Zombie Theme Is Declared, Not Tested

The Problem: The story's core message is "humans and zombies must unite." But Nina already accepts zombies from Scene 01. The audience is zombies, so they're not going to be anti-zombie. No character starts prejudiced and changes their mind. The theme is never challenged.

Why It Hurts: A theme that's never tested is a bumper sticker, not a story. If unity is never difficult, achieving it isn't meaningful.

Fix

Make one of the Bunny Humans defect — a guard who starts as an enemy but gradually questions the Uncle's lies. Show the audience what it costs a human to side with zombies: they lose status, safety, community. Or flip it: make Gloomy distrust humans. He's lived alone for years after humans drove him out. Nina isn't his friend — she's the first human he's had to rely on, and it terrifies him. Now the bridge scene isn't just magical — it's two characters overcoming real prejudice. The hand-touch isn't destiny. It's a choice.

MAJOR

The Audience Has No Real Agency Until the End

The Problem: The audience is told they're "essential" and "not spectators." But narratively, they follow Nina and Gloomy for 80 minutes. They grab arrows, steer a raft, copy movements — but none of these change the story. The first moment their action truly matters is the final hand-raising. That's too late.

Why It Hurts: If the audience's climactic action is meant to feel like a culmination, it needs to be the peak of a curve — not a flat line followed by a spike.

Fix

Build a ladder of agency. Scene 02: the audience must choose to follow Gloomy (he waits — if no one moves, he looks back). Scene 03: the human-walking training isn't just choreography — one person has to step forward first. Scene 06: Pacha's teaching works only if the audience participates genuinely. Each interaction should increase the audience's ownership of the outcome. By Scene 08, raising their hands isn't a new action — it's the final step in a progression they've been building all along.

OPPORTUNITY

Gloomy and Nina's Relationship Is Told, Not Shown

The Problem: We're told their connection is "simple and instinctive, like two children." The drawings on Gloomy's walls tell us he cares. But their actual on-screen interactions are: she throws him to the floor, recognises him, they touch hands, she tells him what to do. That's not a relationship — it's a plot convenience.

Fix

Give them a shared ritual — something small and specific they used to do together before the world fell apart. Maybe they used to watch fireflies. When they reunite in Scene 05, Nina doesn't just "recognise" him — she sees him do the gesture, the one from before, and it breaks her. On the bridge, the flowers that bloom aren't random magic — they're the fireflies' echo. Now the audience understands what's at stake: not just the world, but this specific, fragile thing between two people.

OPPORTUNITY

Pacha Is an Exposition Machine

The Problem: Pacha exists to explain the sun mechanic. She appears, delivers the key information ("the sun is alive underground, humans and zombies must work together"), teaches the hand gesture, and then... the Uncle attacks and she's gone. She has no personal stake, no cost, no dilemma.

Fix

Pacha knows the sun can be woken — because she tried once before, alone, and failed. The failure cost her: that's why she's fused with the roots, why she moves so slowly, why she falls asleep. She's not ancient from age — she's ancient from sacrifice. When the Uncle arrives, Pacha doesn't just get attacked — she makes a choice. She channels the last of her energy into Gloomy before the Uncle can drain all of it. She saves the seed of light at the cost of her own remaining vitality. Now her scene has weight. She's not a tutorial NPC. She's a character who gave everything.

OPPORTUNITY

The Ending Has No Cost

The Problem: Gloomy is resurrected. The Uncle is defeated. The sun rises. Birds sing. Everyone wins. Nothing is permanently lost. The Gravekeeper's closing line — "For now" — gestures at fragility, but the actual events are an unambiguous happy ending.

Fix

Something needs to stay broken. Maybe Gloomy comes back, but not entirely — he's slower, quieter, he lost something in the abyss. Maybe Pacha doesn't survive. Maybe the sun rises, but only over part of the world — the rest is still dark, still waiting. The Gravekeeper's "For now" should feel like a real warning, not a poetic flourish. The best endings don't resolve everything. They resolve enough to give hope, and leave enough unresolved to give the story teeth.

OPPORTUNITY

The Gravekeeper Needs Skin in the Game

The Problem: The Gravekeeper narrates beautifully. He opens and closes the story. He delivers key info in Scene 03. But he has no personal investment. He's a tour guide with a poet's vocabulary. Why does he care if the sun returns?

Fix

Make the Gravekeeper complicit. He knew the sun could be woken — years ago — and chose not to act because he was afraid of what it would cost. He buried the knowledge along with the dead. His guilt is why he tends graves. Now, guiding the audience isn't just narration — it's atonement. His final line hits differently when you know he's the one who could have prevented all of this. "This world is fragile" becomes a confession, not a fortune cookie.

OPPORTUNITY

Scene 07 Is Narratively Hollow

The Problem: "The world shrinks into miniature — portals open and close across forests, cliffs, valleys, ruins — then expands back to full scale." This reads as a spectacular visual set piece, but nothing actually happens story-wise. Gloomy is "gone," Nina keeps walking, the Uncle shows up (again). It's padding between the crisis of Scene 06 and the climax of Scene 08.

Fix

Scene 07 should be the emotional bottom. Not just "Gloomy is gone" — the group fractures. Nina breaks down. The miniature world isn't a visual gimmick — it's the world literally dying, shrinking, running out of time. This is where the audience must decide to keep going WITHOUT a leader. Nina isn't leading them anymore; she's collapsed. A zombie from The Last Standing has to step forward. Give the audience their first moment of genuine leadership before the climax demands it.

Rebuilding the Story

Solutions

You've been living with these characters for 8 years — VR, game, now LBE. You know who they are. The problem is: 95% of your audience doesn't. What lives in your head doesn't exist until it's on the floor, in the room, in 90 minutes. These solutions range from surgical tweaks to structural rewrites. Everything is on the table.

The Curse of Knowledge

You feel like Gloomy, Nina, and the Uncle are fully formed characters. They are — in your heads. But in this script, they arrive pre-built. Gloomy is brave from the start. Nina is empathetic from the start. The Uncle is evil from the start. Nobody changes. Nobody earns who they become. The audience isn't meeting characters — they're meeting statues. Beautiful statues, but statues.

The fix isn't adding backstory or dialogue. It's giving each character a wrong belief that breaks during the experience. That's what an arc is: someone who believes X, gets tested, and ends believing Y. Right now, nobody's beliefs are tested. Everyone is right from Scene 01.

Character Surgery
☠

Gloomy — From Brave Zombie to Frightened Survivor

Major Rewrite
Current

Gloomy is quiet, brave, and selfless from the moment he appears. He drops from above, scatters soldiers, leads the group, fights the Uncle, sacrifices himself. He never hesitates, never fails, never reveals an internal conflict.

Wrong Belief

"I can only protect others by staying alone. Connection is what got everyone killed last time."

Proposed Arc
01
Scene 02 — First meeting is tense, not heroic.

Gloomy doesn't drop from the sky like a savior. He watches the group from a distance first. He leads them to safety, but keeps physical distance. He flinches when someone gets too close. His drawings of Nina on the walls? They're not love letters — they're obsessive attempts to remember the one person he failed to protect. He drew her because he thinks she's dead.

02
Scene 03 — The training reveals his fear.

During the "walk like a human" training, Gloomy can't do it. Not because he's bad at mimicry — because it requires trusting the group, moving in sync, being part of something. The Wild Boar nudges him. The Gravekeeper has to coax him. This is the first crack: he wants to help but he's paralyzed by the last time he tried and failed.

03
Scene 05 — Nina's return breaks him open.

When he frees Nina and she throws him to the floor, he doesn't fight back — he curls up. He expected this. He thinks he deserves it. When she recognizes him, his shock is real: she's alive, she doesn't hate him, and she's reaching for his hand. The bridge scene becomes the moment he chooses connection over safety for the first time in years. The light that spreads isn't magic — it's what happens when he stops hiding.

04
Scene 06 — His sacrifice is a choice, not a reflex.

When the Uncle drains his eyes, it's not a random attack — Gloomy steps in front of Nina. Deliberately. For the first time, he chooses to protect someone knowing the cost. His collapse isn't just physical damage. It's the culmination of his arc: the zombie who was afraid to connect chose to sacrifice himself for connection. Now his death has weight.

05
Scene 08 — His return earns the emotion.

Gloomy doesn't fall through a portal. He walks in. Slowly. Eyes dark. The audience recognizes the walk — the same fearful, distant walk from Scene 02. But this time, he walks toward them. He walks toward Nina. He walks toward the fight. His eyes ignite. The audience understands: he came back because he chose to, not because magic brought him back. The firefly resurrection isn't a plot device — it's the world responding to his transformation.

Result

Before: A brave zombie who stays brave. After: A traumatized survivor who learns that the thing he's most afraid of — connection — is the only thing that can save him and everyone else. His silence goes from being "mysterious" to being the scar of someone who stopped believing anyone could hear him.

✦

Nina — From Perfect Rebel to Broken Idealist

Major Rewrite
Current

Nina is brave, empathetic, and morally perfect from Scene 01 to Outro. She frees the prisoners, defies the Uncle, rescues everyone, leads the final awakening. She never doubts, never fails, never makes a wrong choice. She's a thesis statement in a leather jacket.

Wrong Belief

"I can fix this alone. I don't need anyone — I just need to be brave enough."

Proposed Arc
01
Scene 01 — Her escape has a cost.

Nina opens the door. But her plan is reckless — she didn't think it through. In the chaos, not every prisoner makes it out. She hears the screams behind her as the door closes. She says "Someone is waiting for you above" — but her voice cracks. She got people killed. Her bravery wasn't enough. This guilt follows her.

02
Scene 04 — She's harder than before.

When the audience meets Nina again after her capture, she's changed. The Uncle's imprisonment didn't just contain her — it broke something. She's colder, more tactical, less trusting. When she throws Gloomy to the floor, it's not a misunderstanding — it's rage. She's been betrayed, abandoned, and trapped. She's not the same girl from Scene 01. The audience should feel the distance.

03
Scene 05 — The bridge forces vulnerability.

On the broken bridge, Nina doesn't want to take Gloomy's hand. She's done trusting. She's done being soft. But the bridge won't rebuild without both of them. Physically, mechanically, the world demands that she choose to connect. When she finally reaches back — it costs her. The light that spreads is her wall breaking, not just magic happening.

04
Scene 07 — She falls apart.

"Gloomy is gone." In the current script, Nina "keeps moving." That's wrong. She should collapse. She should stop. The one person she let back in is dead because of her — again. She failed — again. This is where the audience must step forward without her. The Last Standing lead for the first time. Not because they're heroes, but because Nina can't anymore.

05
Scene 08 — "Do it" means something different now.

When Nina raises her hands and says "Do it! What you learned. Now." — it's not a commander giving orders. It's someone who finally understands that she can't do this alone. The whole experience has been teaching her that individual bravery isn't enough. The hand-raising is her admitting she needs them. All of them. That vulnerability — not courage — is what actually saves the world.

Result

Before: A brave rebel who stays brave. After: An idealist who gets broken by the cost of her own heroism, hardens, and has to learn that the vulnerability she's been running from is the only thing that can actually save the world. Her arc mirrors Gloomy's: both afraid of connection for different reasons, both forced to choose it.

★

The Uncle — From Cartoon Tyrant to Tragic Architect

Major Rewrite
Current

The Uncle is evil, narcissistic, paranoid, and obsessed with control. He screams orders, kicks soldiers off trains, and drains light from zombies. He's a walking threat level with no internal life. He appears in almost every scene like a video game boss who keeps respawning.

Wrong Belief

"If I control everything, I can undo what I did. If I am the sun, no one will know I killed the real one."

Proposed Arc — Option A: The Architect
A1
He caused the sun to retreat.

Years ago, the Uncle wasn't a tyrant — he was a scientist, an engineer, someone who believed he could harness the sun's energy for humanity. His experiment failed catastrophically. The sun didn't just dim — it fled underground to survive. Zombies emerged. The world collapsed. And the man responsible built a regime to cover it up.

A2
His device isn't a weapon — it's a failed fix.

The device that extracts sun energy? He built it to try to bring the sun back. But it doesn't work — it only drains, never restores. He keeps trying, keeps failing, and blames the zombies for "corrupting" the energy. His zombie hatred is projection. They're not the disease — he is. He just can't admit it.

A3
Nina is his daughter (or the closest thing to it).

Give the relationship a specific shape. Nina is the daughter of his former colleague — the one who died in the experiment. He took Nina in out of guilt. His possessiveness isn't random — it's the last thread connecting him to the person he used to be. He can't lose Nina because losing her means admitting there's nothing good left in him.

A4
His defeat should make the audience feel something.

When Gloomy throws him into the abyss, there should be a beat — half a second — where the Uncle looks at Nina and she sees who he used to be. Not forgiveness. Not redemption. Just recognition: this monster was once a person who tried to help and failed so badly that he destroyed the world rather than face what he'd done. His fall is tragic, not triumphant.

Proposed Arc — Option B: The Simpler Fix
B1
If you don't want to rewrite his backstory: reduce his appearances.

The Uncle should appear in maximum 3 scenes: Scene 01 (establish threat), Scene 04 (the train — his domain), and Scene 08 (final confrontation). Remove him from Scenes 06 and 07. Send his lieutenants or his pets instead. Every time the Uncle appears, it should feel like the ground shifts. Right now, he's wallpaper.

B2
Give him one human moment.

Even without a full backstory rewrite, add a single beat on the train where the Uncle looks at his extraction device and the audience catches something — frustration? Sadness? The device isn't working the way he needs it to. He's not just angry — he's desperate. One crack in the armor transforms a cartoon into a character. You don't need a monologue. You need three seconds of silence where his mask slips.

Result

Before: An obstacle with a face. After (Option A): The story's original sin made flesh — a man who broke the world and built an empire to avoid facing it. His defeat is sad, not just satisfying. After (Option B): Same villain, but with less screen time and one moment of humanity that makes his defeat land harder.

▦

The Gravekeeper — From Tour Guide to Guilty Witness

Moderate Rewrite
Current

He narrates the opening, delivers news in Scene 03, teaches choreography, and closes the story. He's elegant, poetic, and completely detached. He has no personal stake. He's a well-dressed Wikipedia article.

Wrong Belief

"Observing is enough. Recording is enough. I don't need to act — I just need to remember."

Proposed Arc
01
He knew how to wake the sun — and chose not to.

The Gravekeeper discovered the same knowledge Pacha holds — years ago. But acting on it required risk: humans and zombies together, a ritual that might fail, a confrontation with the Uncle. He chose to bury the knowledge. Literally — he became a gravekeeper, tending the dead instead of fighting for the living. His poetry isn't wisdom. It's avoidance dressed up in beautiful language.

02
Scene 03 — The choreography training is his confession.

When he teaches the group to walk like humans, there's a moment where he almost says something — then stops. He knows more than he's telling. His "enigmatic tone" isn't a personality trait — it's cowardice. He's been rehearsing this moment for years: the moment he finally helps someone act, instead of just watching.

03
Outro — His final line is a confession, not a summary.

"This world is fragile. It always was. But today, it lives. For now." If the audience knows his history, this line transforms. He's not narrating the ending — he's admitting that every year the world stayed dark was a year he could have acted and didn't. "For now" isn't poetic ambiguity — it's his promise that this time, he won't look away.

❀

Pacha — From Exposition to Sacrifice

Moderate Rewrite
Current

Pacha delivers the most important plot information in the story (how to wake the sun), teaches the gesture, and then gets attacked by the Uncle. She has zero personal conflict. She exists to explain the rules.

Proposed Change
01
Pacha tried to wake the sun before — alone — and it destroyed her.

She's not ancient from age. She's ancient from sacrifice. She attempted the ritual by herself, without the human-zombie bond, and the backlash fused her to the forest. Her slowness isn't quaint — it's damage. Her falling asleep isn't funny — it's her body shutting down. She knows the ritual works because she almost died proving it. And she knows it requires both sides because her failure was trying to do it alone.

02
When the Uncle attacks, Pacha makes a choice.

In the current script, the Uncle "drains Gloomy's eyes" and Pacha... is just there. Instead: Pacha sees the Uncle coming and has a split second to choose. She can protect herself — retreat into the roots, survive, wait for another chance in another century. Or she can pour her remaining energy into Gloomy, planting the seed of light that will let him return in Scene 08. She chooses Gloomy. She chooses these people. She chooses now. It costs her everything. The audience should understand: this ancient being just died for them.

Result

Before: A tutorial NPC. After: A character who tried to save the world alone, failed, paid the ultimate price, and in her final act chose to give everything she had left to make sure this group succeeds where she couldn't. Her sacrifice gives the ending its cost.

⚙

The Fresh Eyes Problem — What the Audience Actually Receives

Critical Awareness
What You Think the Audience Knows

Gloomy and Nina have a deep history. The Uncle has a complex relationship with Nina. The world has rich lore. The zombies are sympathetic beings. The sun has a metaphysical connection to the forest. The fireflies are culturally significant.

What the Audience Actually Knows After 90 Minutes

A zombie saved me. A girl helped me escape. A bad man is chasing us. We need to raise our hands at the end. That's it. Everything else is atmosphere — beautiful, immersive atmosphere, but not story.

The Rule
!
If a character trait isn't demonstrated through a choice that costs something, it doesn't exist.

You can't tell the audience Gloomy is brave — he has to do something that scares him. You can't tell them Nina is empathetic — she has to choose empathy when cruelty would be easier. You can't tell them the Uncle is complex — he has to show a crack. In an LBE, you don't have dialogue or internal monologue. You have ACTION. Every character trait must be expressed through a visible, physical choice with visible consequences.

!
Test everything with the "cold audience" filter.

For every scene, ask: "If someone who has never heard of Gloomy Eyes walks in right now, what do they understand?" If the answer depends on knowledge from the VR or the game, it's not in the experience. Write it into the experience or cut it. There is no middle ground.

◆

Portal System — Rules or Removal

Moderate Rewrite
Option A: Establish Rules
A

Portals are scars left by the sun's retreat — cracks in reality. They're natural, unpredictable, and dangerous. Establish them visually in the Intro (the Gravekeeper's puppet show shows the sun leaving and cracks forming). When a portal appears in Scene 05, the audience already understands what it is. Make them cost something: every time someone steps through, they lose something — a memory, warmth, time. By Scene 07, when portals are everywhere, the world is literally falling apart at the seams. The portals aren't escape routes — they're symptoms of the world dying.

Option B: Remove Them
B

Cut portals entirely. Scene transitions happen through physical movement — tunnels, rivers, doors, paths. Scene 08: Gloomy doesn't fall from a portal, he walks in from the darkness. This is an LBE — the audience is physically moving through space. Use that. Every transition should feel like the world is leading them somewhere, not teleporting them. Portals belong in VR and games. In a physical space, the journey IS the story.

♾

The Agency Ladder — Teaching the Audience to Act

Major Rewrite
Current

The audience follows for 80 minutes, then raises their hands. The final action should feel like a culmination, not a surprise request.

Proposed Ladder
01
Scene 01: Passive — Be freed.

Nina opens the door. The audience just has to walk through. This is correct — don't change it. The first action should be simple: accept help.

02
Scene 02: Reactive — Follow Gloomy.

But add a beat where Gloomy waits. He doesn't just lead — he stops, looks back, and waits for the group to choose to follow. If nobody moves, he takes a step back toward them. The audience learns: this character needs US to act, not just the other way around.

03
Scene 03: Cooperative — Train together.

The choreography isn't just copying — one person has to step forward first. The Gravekeeper doesn't pick someone. He waits. The group has to self-organize. This is their first experience of collective decision-making.

04
Scene 06: Active — Learn the ritual.

Pacha's hand-raising practice isn't a demo — it only works when enough people participate. If half the room isn't doing it, the fireflies dim. The audience learns: half-effort = half-result. Full commitment = full magic.

05
Scene 07: Leadership — Carry on without Nina.

Nina collapses. No one is leading. The glowing footprints are faint. Someone in the audience has to walk first. This is the hardest moment — not physically, but psychologically. The audience becomes the protagonist for the first time.

06
Scene 08: Culmination — Awaken the sun.

Now the hand-raising isn't a surprise. It's the final step in a progression: be freed → follow → cooperate → practice → lead → save. Every previous interaction trained them for this moment. The audience doesn't just raise their hands because Nina says to — they raise them because they've been building toward this action for 85 minutes.

⚠

Scene 07 — The Emotional Bottom That Isn't

Moderate Rewrite
Current

"Gloomy is gone. Nina keeps moving. The world shrinks to miniature." It's visually interesting but emotionally flat. Nothing actually happens in this scene except walking and looking at things. It's a screensaver between the crisis and the climax.

Proposed Rewrite
07
This scene should be the hardest 10 minutes of the experience.

Nina stops. She sits down. She's done. The Gravekeeper appears — but this time, he doesn't narrate. He sits beside her. Silence. The miniature world isn't a spectacle — it's the world dying. Shrinking. Getting smaller because it's running out of energy to hold itself together. The glowing footprints aren't Gloomy's — they're the last traces of the sun, fading. The audience has to choose: wait for someone to lead, or walk into the dark themselves. No music. No narration. Just the sound of the mountain beginning to crack. Someone takes the first step. Others follow. Nina watches them go, then stands. Not because she's brave again — because she can't let them go alone.

☀

The Ending — What Stays Broken

Small Tweak
Three Options
A
Pacha doesn't survive.

If she sacrifices herself in Scene 06, her absence in the Outro is the cost. The sun rises, but the person who made it possible is gone. The Gravekeeper's "For now" becomes a eulogy.

B
Gloomy returns, but changed.

He comes back. But he's slower. His eyes glow faintly, not brightly. He gave something permanent to bring the sun back. The audience understands: miracles have a price. Joy and grief in the same breath.

C
The sun rises — but only here.

Light spreads across the Alishan mountain. But on the horizon, darkness remains. The world isn't saved — one corner of it is. The work isn't done. The audience leaves knowing this was a beginning, not a conclusion. "For now" becomes a call to action, not a caveat.

Participation

You Are Not a Spectator

The Last Standing is an audience-driven experience. Every action you take has consequence. The world responds to what you do — together.

▶

Movement Choreography

Mirror the Gravekeeper's movements precisely to pass as human through enemy territory. Get it wrong, and the disguise fails.

✨

Light Particle Control

Raise and lower your hands in sync with the forest's energy. Your gestures directly activate hidden light within the natural world.

→

Arrow Manipulation

Grab arrows mid-flight, pull them out, and throw them. Zombies feel no pain — use that to your advantage.

♨

Raft Navigation

Shift your weight to guide the raft through underground river passages toward the light of the Alishan forest.

⚘

Flower Triggers

Touch the flowers that drift past on the broken bridge. Each contact releases a cascade of petals — a moment of magic between Gloomy and Nina.

☀

The Final Awakening

The most important action in the experience: raise your hands together with Nina. Without the collective motion of every single person, the sun stays buried forever.

Rooted in Place

Taiwan Cultural Elements

Gloomy Eyes is deeply embedded in the landscapes, wildlife, infrastructure, and indigenous heritage of Taiwan — specifically the Alishan mountain region.

🌳

Alishan Forest

The primary landscape draws from Alishan — Taiwan's iconic high-altitude forest of giant cypress trees, layered canopy, mountain presence, and characteristic mountain fog.

🚂

Alishan Forest Railway

The iconic red mountain trains of the Alishan Forest Railway — a historic narrow-gauge line built during the Japanese colonial era — serve as the setting for Scene 4.

🦅

Formosan Wildlife

Gloomy's wild boar references the Formosan wild boar. The Uncle's pets are inspired by the Formosan leopard cat, one of Taiwan's rarest native predators.

🏭

Tsou Indigenous Heritage

Pacha's wardrobe is drawn from the Tsou people — indigenous to the Alishan region. Her cave is inspired by Alishan's Three Generation Tree.

🌎

Sound & Atmosphere

Bird sounds inspired by native Taiwanese species. Interior mountain spaces reflect Taiwan's geological formations. A soundscape grounded in a deeply specific, real place.

🎄

Fireflies

Fireflies appear as a recurring motif throughout the experience. Firefly watching is a beloved cultural tradition in Taiwan's mountain forests, particularly around Alishan each spring.
